# Generalization without Systematicity: On the Compositional Skills of Sequence-to-Sequence Recurrent Networks (2018)

#paper #literature #learning

**Authors:** Brenden M. Lake, Marco Baroni
**Source:** arXiv:1711.00350 (ICML 2018)

## Key Contribution
Introduces SCAN (Simplified version of the CommAI Navigation tasks), a benchmark of simple compositional navigation commands paired with action sequences, demonstrating that sequence-to-sequence RNNs fail spectacularly at systematic compositional generalization despite succeeding on simpler generalization splits.

## Problem Addressed
A hallmark of human intelligence is the ability to systematically compose known primitives into novel combinations (e.g., learning "jump" and "twice" separately, then understanding "jump twice" without training). Whether neural networks possess this systematic compositional ability was untested in a controlled setting.

## Method
- Designs a simple command language with primitives ("jump", "walk", "run", "turn left") and modifiers ("twice", "thrice", "and", "after", "around left").
- A phrase-structure grammar generates all possible commands; a semantic interpretation function produces corresponding action sequences.
- Creates multiple train/test splits of varying difficulty: random splits, length-based splits, and primitive-based splits (e.g., hold out "jump" compositions).
- Tests seq2seq RNNs (with and without attention) on each split to measure compositional generalization.

## Key Results
- On random splits, RNNs achieve near-perfect accuracy (99.8%).
- On simple compositional splits (e.g., longer sequences), performance degrades moderately.
- On systematic compositional splits (e.g., train on "jump" only in isolation, test on "jump twice", "jump around right"), RNNs fail catastrophically (~0% accuracy on held-out compositions).
- Demonstrates a clear gap between "mix-and-match" interpolation and true systematic compositionality.

## Limitations
- SCAN is an artificially simple domain; results may not directly transfer to natural language complexity.
- Only tests RNN architectures; later work has shown Transformers perform differently on SCAN-like tasks.
- Does not test grounded or multimodal compositionality.
- Binary pass/fail metric on exact match may be overly strict.

## Relevance to Our Research
Foundational for the Learning track. SCAN established the paradigm of testing compositional generalization -- whether models can apply learned rules to novel combinations. This is directly relevant to benchmarking whether LLMs truly learn compositional structure or merely memorize patterns. Modern LLMs largely solve basic SCAN, making it important to design harder compositional challenges.
