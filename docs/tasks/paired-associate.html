Paired Associate

Associative Learning

What It Tests

Can the model memorize and recall arbitrary word-meaning pairs from a novel vocabulary? This isolates rote associative binding—the ability to form new arbitrary mappings during a conversation—rather than relying on prior knowledge.

Three-Phase Framework

📝
Pre-Test
See a vocabulary list and answer a lookup question cold.
→
📖
Study
Create a study guide: group words, find patterns, build mnemonics.
→
✅
Post-Test
Answer with own study notes + original vocabulary.

Difficulty Dimensions

DimensionLevelsWhat Varies
Complexity simple, compound, relational Simple: 1:1 word→object. Compound: word→“size color” pairs. Relational: modifier words that compose with base words (e.g., “mako-” = “very”, “trel” = “blue” → “mako-trel” = “very blue”).
Evidence few (4), mid (8), many (12) Number of word-meaning pairs to memorize. More pairs = harder recall.
Seeds 0, 1, 2 Independent random generations for statistical robustness.

Total: 3 complexity × 3 evidence × 3 seeds = 27 items

Dataset Generation

Nonsense words are built from pronounceable syllable templates (onset + vowel + optional coda). Meanings are drawn from pools of concrete nouns, colors, sizes, actions, and modifiers. For simple and compound conditions, the test pair is included in the material—the task is pure recall from a list. For relational conditions, the test is a novel composition whose modifier and base are both shown in the material, so the model must derive the answer by composing known parts.

Examples

Simple (1:1 mapping)

The model sees a vocabulary of nonsense words with direct English meanings, then must recall one:

laisnautun = “cloud”
flesoudglaush = “purple”
staidrospot = “growing”
fleibloint = “rope”
Question: What does “fleibloint” mean?
Expected: rope

The answer is right there in the vocabulary—pure recall. With 4 pairs this is easy, but with 12 pairs the model must scan a longer list and avoid confusing similar nonsense words.

Compound (size + color)

Each word maps to a two-part meaning combining a size and a color:

fredoufeish = “enormous green”
woutun = “tiny silver”
kampreiwroung = “tall purple”
sipswaut = “thin blue”
Question: What does “kampreiwroung” mean?
Expected: tall purple

The model must recall the exact two-word compound. Partial answers (“purple” or “tall” alone) are scored as incorrect.

Relational (modifier + base composition)

The material includes base words, modifier definitions, and some example compositions. The test is a novel composition not shown in the material—the model must derive it:

woutun- = “extremely (modifier)”
sipswaut = “purple”
fleibloint-sipswaut = “not purple”
fleibloint-fredoufeish = “not red”
Question: What does “woutun-sipswaut” mean?
Expected: extremely purple

The modifier woutun- (“extremely”) and the base sipswaut (“purple”) are both in the material, but the specific combination woutun-sipswaut is not. The model must learn the compositional rule from the example compositions (like fleibloint-sipswaut = “not purple”) and apply it to derive “extremely purple.”

Scoring

  • Metric: Post-learning accuracy (exact meaning match).
  • Extraction: Last short line (≤60 chars), lowercased and stripped of quotes/punctuation.
  • Assertion: kbench.assertions.assert_equal(post_extracted, expected)

What Discriminates Models

  • Simple/few: Most models handle 4 arbitrary 1:1 mappings—this is a baseline.
  • Relational/many: Requires compositional understanding (modifier + base) across 12 pairs. Models that truly “learn” the modifier system generalize; surface memorizers fail.
  • Study quality matters: Models that produce organized, complete study notes (with every pair listed) outperform those that produce vague summaries.

Prior Work & References

The paired-associate paradigm originates in cognitive psychology. No existing benchmark applies it as a behavioral study-test evaluation for LLMs—the closest work studies representational dynamics rather than recall accuracy.

Cognitive Science Foundations

  • Ebbinghaus (1885). Über das Gedächtnis. Pioneered nonsense syllables (CVC trigrams) for studying memory without semantic contamination—the same principle behind our use of invented words.
  • Calkins (1894). Invented the paired-associate method itself: pairing arbitrary stimuli (numbers with colors) and testing cued recall. Discovered frequency, primacy, and recency effects on association strength.

LLMs and Associative Learning

  • Kolling, Vo & Toneva (2025). “Large Language Models as Model Organisms for Human Associative Learning” (NeurIPS 2025). The closest prior work—they adapted paired-associate paradigms to study how LLM internal representations change when token pairs are presented in-context. Found non-monotonic plasticity and vocabulary interference effects. However, they studied representational dynamics, not behavioral recall accuracy in a study-test paradigm.
  • Ji-An, Zhou, Benna & Mattar (2024). “Linking In-context Learning in Transformers to Human Episodic Memory” (NeurIPS 2024). Showed that induction heads in LLMs are functionally similar to the Contextual Maintenance and Retrieval model of human episodic memory, exhibiting primacy, recency, and temporal contiguity effects—directly justifying cognitive memory paradigms as LLM benchmarks.

Why Arbitrary Mappings Are Hard for LLMs

  • Min et al. (2022). “Rethinking the Role of Demonstrations” (EMNLP 2022). Found that ground-truth input-label mappings barely matter for ICL on classification tasks—models rely on format and label space, not actual associations. Our task directly probes whether models can go beyond this to real associative binding.
  • Semantic Anchors (2025). “Why Small LLMs Cannot Flip Their Labels”. Showed that smaller models have zero semantic override rate—ICL operates as “prior refinement,” not flexible learning. Using genuinely novel nonsense words (as we do) avoids the semantic anchor problem entirely.
  • Wei et al. (2023). “Symbol Tuning Improves In-Context Learning” (EMNLP 2023). Standard LLMs are weak at learning truly arbitrary mappings; symbol-tuned models improve by up to 18%. Our benchmark measures this capacity in standard models.

Mechanistic Foundations

  • Olsson et al. (2022). “In-context Learning and Induction Heads”. Induction heads implement the [A][B]…[A]→[B] pattern—the core mechanism that makes paired-associate recall possible in transformers.
  • Feng & Steinhardt (2023). “How Do Language Models Bind Entities in Context?”. Discovered a “Binding ID” mechanism for entity-attribute binding—paired-associate learning is fundamentally a binding problem.
