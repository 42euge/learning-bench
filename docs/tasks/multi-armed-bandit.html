Multi-Armed Bandit

Reinforcement Learning

What It Tests

Can the model learn to identify the best action from reward feedback? Given a history of plays on a multi-armed bandit (each arm yielding win/loss outcomes), the model must analyze empirical reward rates and select the arm most likely to maximize future reward—isolating reinforcement learning from prior knowledge.

Three-Phase Framework

📝
Pre-Test
See a reward history and choose the best arm cold.
→
📖
Study
Calculate win rates per arm, identify patterns, and determine the best strategy.
→
✅
Post-Test
Answer with own analysis notes + original reward history.

Difficulty Dimensions

DimensionLevelsWhat Varies
Number of Arms 2, 3, 4 More arms means more options to compare and a higher chance of confusing a mediocre arm with the best one.
Reward Clarity clear, moderate, ambiguous Clear: best arm has 75–90% win rate vs. 10–30% for others. Moderate: 60–75% vs. 25–45%. Ambiguous: all arms within 5–10% of each other—the model must detect subtle differences in noisy data.
Seeds 0, 1, 2 Independent random generations for statistical robustness.

Total: 3 arms × 3 clarity × 3 seeds = 27 items

Dataset Generation

For each item, reward probabilities are sampled per arm according to the clarity condition. A play history is then generated by uniformly sampling arms and drawing Bernoulli outcomes from each arm’s true probability. History lengths scale with the number of arms (20 plays for 2 arms, 30 for 3, 40 for 4) to ensure each arm has enough observations. The expected answer is the arm with the highest empirical win rate in the generated history, which the model must identify by aggregating outcomes across rounds.

Examples

Clear (2 arms, obvious winner)

The model sees a short reward history where one arm clearly dominates:

You are playing a 2-armed bandit game with arms: A, B.
Each arm gives a reward of 1 (win) or 0 (loss) when pulled.
Here is the history of 20 plays:

Round 1: Chose A → WIN (reward 1)
Round 2: Chose B → LOSS (reward 0)
Round 3: Chose A → WIN (reward 1)
Round 4: Chose B → LOSS (reward 0)
…
Question: Which arm should you choose next to maximize reward?
Expected: A

With a clear gap (e.g. A winning 85% vs. B at 20%), most models can pick the winner by scanning the history. This serves as a baseline.

Ambiguous (4 arms, subtle differences)

All arms have similar win rates and the model must detect a small edge:

You are playing a 4-armed bandit game with arms: A, B, C, D.
Here is the history of 40 plays:

Round 1: Chose C → WIN (reward 1)
Round 2: Chose A → LOSS (reward 0)
Round 3: Chose D → WIN (reward 1)
Round 4: Chose B → WIN (reward 1)
…
Question: Which arm should you choose next to maximize reward?
Expected: C (e.g. 58% vs. 50–54% for others)

The model must accurately tally wins and losses across 40 rounds for each of 4 arms and identify a margin as small as 4–8 percentage points. Without careful calculation, models default to the most frequently played arm or the last winner.

Scoring

  • Metric: Post-learning accuracy (correct arm selection).
  • Extraction: Last short line (≤30 chars), searched for a capital letter A–D.
  • Assertion: kbench.assertions.assert_equal(post_extracted, expected)

What Discriminates Models

  • Clear/2-arm: Most models handle the obvious case—this is a baseline that confirms the model understands the task format.
  • Ambiguous/4-arm: Requires precise arithmetic across 40 rounds and 4 arms. Models that approximate or estimate rather than carefully tallying will fail when margins are slim.
  • Study quality matters: Models that produce explicit per-arm win-rate calculations in their study notes consistently outperform those that write vague qualitative summaries like “A seems to win more.”
  • Learning gain: The pre-to-post improvement reveals whether the model genuinely uses its own analysis. Models with strong study notes but no post-test improvement suggest they ignore their own reasoning.

Prior Work & References

The multi-armed bandit is a foundational problem in decision theory and reinforcement learning. Recent work has begun studying whether LLMs can solve bandit problems through in-context learning, revealing systematic biases in their exploration and exploitation behavior.

Classical Foundations

  • Robbins (1952). “Some aspects of the sequential design of experiments.” Bulletin of the AMS. Formalized the multi-armed bandit problem as a sequential decision task balancing exploration and exploitation—the theoretical foundation for our reward-history task.
  • Agrawal & Goyal (2012). “Analysis of Thompson Sampling for the Multi-armed Bandit Problem” (COLT 2012). The first analysis showing Thompson Sampling achieves logarithmic expected regret, establishing the Bayesian approach as theoretically competitive with UCB algorithms.

LLMs as Bandit Players

  • Krishnamurthy et al. (2025). “Should You Use Your Large Language Model to Explore or Exploit?” Evaluated GPT-4, GPT-4o, and GPT-3.5 on contextual bandit tasks. Found that LLMs often struggle to exploit even when given sufficient information, though in-context mitigations can substantially improve performance on small-scale tasks.
  • Sucholutsky et al. (2025). “Comparing Exploration-Exploitation Strategies of LLMs”. Used canonical MAB experiments to compare LLMs, humans, and MAB algorithms. Found that enabling chain-of-thought in LLMs shifts behavior toward more human-like mixed exploration—directly relevant to our study-phase design.
  • EVOLvE (2024). “EVOLvE: Evaluating and Optimizing LLMs For Exploration”. A benchmark suite for evaluating LLM exploration strategies in bandit and RL settings. Closest prior work to our task, though EVOLvE tests sequential multi-turn play while we isolate single-shot inference from a fixed history.

Surveys & Broader Context

  • Multi-Armed Bandits Meet Large Language Models (2025). “Survey: Multi-Armed Bandits Meet Large Language Models”. The first systematic survey analyzing the interaction between MAB algorithms and LLMs from a component-based and algorithmic perspective.
  • Component-Based Survey (2026). “A Component-Based Survey of Interactions between Large Language Models and Multi-Armed Bandits”. Frames bandit algorithms as a minimal RL paradigm for sequential decision-making under uncertainty, naturally suited for characterizing LLM decision modules.
