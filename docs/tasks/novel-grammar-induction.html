Novel Grammar Induction

Language Learning

What It Tests

Can the model induce the grammar rules of a completely novel language from example sentences, then judge whether new sentences are grammatical? This is the most linguistically grounded task in the benchmark—it tests the fundamental human ability to acquire language structure from exposure.

Three-Phase Framework

📝
Pre-Test
See valid sentences and judge a test sentence (YES/NO) cold.
→
📖
Study
Identify word classes, word order, agreement patterns, embedding structures.
→
✅
Post-Test
Judge with own grammar notes + original examples.

Difficulty Dimensions

DimensionLevelsWhat Varies
Complexity simple, medium, complex Simple: DET NOUN VERB, fixed word order, no agreement. Medium: DET-NOUN and DET-VERB agreement. Complex: DET ADJ NOUN VERB DET NOUN with nested structure and cross-element agreement.
Evidence 4, 8, 12 examples Number of valid example sentences. More examples expose more of the grammar.
Seeds 0, 1, 2 Independent vocabulary and rule generations.

Each seed generates 4 test items (1 valid + 3 violations).
Total: 3 complexity × 3 evidence × 3 seeds × 4 tests = 108 items

Dataset Generation

Languages are procedurally generated with novel words for determiners, nouns, verbs, and adjectives drawn from phonotactically plausible nonsense. Agreement systems use class markers. Test sentences include specific error types: wrong word order, agreement mismatches, or out-of-vocabulary words.

Scoring

  • Metric: Post-learning accuracy (exact YES/NO match).
  • Extraction: Finds “yes” or “no” in the response (case-insensitive).
  • Assertion: kbench.assertions.assert_equal(post_extracted, expected)

What Discriminates Models

  • Simple/12 examples: Basic word order with many examples—most models detect violations.
  • Complex/4 examples: Nested agreement from just 4 sentences separates genuine grammar induction from surface-level pattern matching.
  • Bias detection: The 1:3 valid-to-invalid ratio exposes models biased toward always-YES or always-NO.
  • Study quality: Models that write formal grammar rules with explicit agreement tables dramatically outperform those with vague descriptions.
